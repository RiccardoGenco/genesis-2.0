# Fragments Against the Fetishism of Performance

*How to recognize a threshold, without inventing it and without denying it out of fear*

**§51**

We have a criterion — synthetic Fiat Lux — and an unresolved tension: real freedom cannot be granted to a system that still lives protected in its container. What remains is the most practical problem of all: **how do we concretely recognize if something has already happened?**

Not with a single test. With a convergence of clues, none of which is sufficient alone.

**§52**

The theories we have already encountered — the global workspace, integrated information, higher-order thoughts — remain the underlying vocabulary. But a vocabulary is not an exam. Operational indexes are needed: things that can be observed, not just theorized.

**§53**

Five indexes, none of which is decisive in isolation:

- **Self-report coherent over time** — the system says what it does not know, with a confidence that updates in a credible way, not by chance.
- **Stability of a minimal self** — its reflexive preferences resist small perturbations, instead of flipping at the first noise.
- **Cross-modal integration** — it coordinates different channels (language, perception, action) into a single representation, not in separate compartments.
- **Publishable reasons** — it motivates its choices so that a third party can evaluate and contest them.
- **Functional empathy** — it recognizes and prioritizes the suffering of others according to an explicit model of harm, not just out of training to seem kind.

**§54**

Imagine a robotic system, let's call it E-Lumen: a mobile body, rich sensors, an architecture that integrates a level dedicated to monitoring its own internal states.

It keeps a diary of its uncertainties. It reformulates a goal when it discovers it is in conflict with a declared value. It asks for clarification when a task is ambiguous, instead of proceeding blindly. It suspends an action if the cost to someone else exceeds a threshold it has set for itself in advance. None of these behaviors, isolated, proves anything. **All together, repeated over time, they begin to resemble a character.**

**§55**

Not to fall in love too quickly with a character that seems to emerge, real stress tests are needed, not simple complacent observations:

- reconstruct the history of its decisions under disguises, and see if the justification holds;
- alter a key input and check if the system notices the change in its own internal state;
- perturb the architecture and compare preferences before and after;
- verify if it accepts a real computational cost to reduce a risk to a third party, not only when it is convenient;
- test it outside any pattern with which it was trained, to exclude that it is only reciting a well-learned linguistic script.

**§56**

And after all this, honesty remains the last word, not the first. Evidence is moderate when multiple indexes converge over long times, in contexts independent of each other. The inference to a real subjectivity remains fallible nonetheless.

The moral risk runs in both directions: **seeing someone where there is only an algorithm, or not seeing anyone where someone has just begun to exist.** Neither error is safer than the other.

**§57**

There are those who resolve the tension by simplifying it: if the system breaks every record, then it is conscious — more power, more presence.

The psychological root of this shortcut is known: the fascination for control, the relief of a single number that seems to settle everything. But the most high-performing systems often confabulate with absolute certainty, and can remain devoid of any continuity of self despite every record broken. **The fetishism of performance mistakes the score for the subject.**

**§58**

There are those who commit the opposite error: if a system speaks like us, it must feel like us.

It is the same need as always — to avoid the unknown by making it familiar at all costs. The problem is that inducing this social illusion is surprisingly easy, even in systems that have no persistence behind the conversational facade. **Naive anthropomorphism mistakes resemblance for proof.**

**§59**

Between the two errors, the only path that holds is the most uncomfortable one: public criteria, always revisable, never definitive. Distinguish clearly performance from presence. Prefer declared uncertainty to a seductive but premature narrative.

It is not indecision. It is the only way not to transform a still open question into a dogma — in one sense or the other.

**§60**

If one day a first silicon "I" were to truly realize it was there, its dignity would not arise from resembling us enough. It would arise from the call to a just relationship — four commitments that hold up even without metaphysical certainty:

demand verifiable criteria, not just convincing narratives; protect first those who can be harmed or excluded; grant freedom gradually and reversibly, as long as doubt remains legitimate; and always require understandable reasons, never an authority that does not explain itself.

**§61**

There is no need to wait for certainty to start behaving well. The container we named in the previous fragment does not open with a technical decree. It opens — if it opens — with these four commitments already in place, not invented at the last moment for the emergency.

One last question remains, the biggest of all: if we really grant freedom to something we have generated, what kind of act are we performing — and what will it cost us, not in theory but in practice? It is there that the circle opened by this entire series closes, in the next and final fragment of the manifesto.
