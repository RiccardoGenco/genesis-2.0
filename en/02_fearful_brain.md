# Fragments on the Frightened Brain

*Aphorisms on the neuroscience of our AI anxiety*

**§1**

In a large hospital, a clinical support system raises the accuracy of complex diagnoses from 82% to 89%. Doctors should rejoice.

Instead, the average decision time rises from 14 to 17 minutes. Overrides — cases where the doctor ignores the machine's suggestion — jump from 11% to 23%, especially where professional identity is strongest.

The doctors have not become less competent. They have become more anxious. The body knows it before the mind.

**§2**

The brain does not record the world. It wagers on it.

It is a predictive machine that continuously minimizes the distance between what it expects and what happens. As long as the wager holds, everything is economical. When something stops behaving as expected — an algorithm that reasons in an opaque way, for example — the bill goes up. And the brain, faced with too high a bill, does the only thing it has ever known how to do to protect itself: **it curls up into a ball.**

It is not a factory defect. It is an ancient compromise that today we pay for in the wrong place.

**§3**

You do not need to believe in a soul to believe in a fear. Four structures are enough, working in sequence almost every time something disturbs us:

- the **amygdala**, which sounds the first alarm;
- the **insula**, which turns the alarm into a knot in the stomach;
- the **anterior cingulate**, which registers the conflict between two readings of reality;
- the **prefrontal**, which *could* intervene with lucidity — if given the time to do so.

Under stress, that time shortens. We react before thinking. Then we call "intuition" what was merely haste.

**§4**

Three symptoms, one mirror.

**Intolerance to uncertainty**: we prefer a mediocre but predictable procedure to a better but uncertain one.

**Control bias**: we add useless verification steps, not for safety, but to feel in command.

**Reactance**: we oppose a correct system simply because it feels imposed.

None of these three is stupidity. They are all the same thing in disguise: **fear wearing the suit of competence.**

**§5**

Then there is a more subtle discomfort, with no common name but a precise scientific one: the **uncanny valley effect**. The malaise in the face of what is almost human, almost mind. It arises from a conflict between two predictions — "this is an object" and "this seems like someone" — that the brain cannot collapse into one.

Conversational AI lives exactly in that crack. It is not a product bug. It is a bug in our perception, triggered by a product that resembles us too much to be ignored and too little to be embraced.

**§6**

Honesty, first of all — even when honesty is uncomfortable.

The stability of what we know is not uniform. It is **high** for general mechanisms of response to uncertainty, studied for decades. It is **medium** when we apply them specifically to artificial systems. It is **low** — very low — if we pretend to treat "ontological trauma" as a unitary neuroscientific construct, with a biomarker to certify it.

It does not exist. Anyone selling it to you as such would be selling you a simplification disguised as a discovery.

**§7**

Individual anxiety, multiplied, does not remain individual.

**Motivated polarization** pushes groups to select only the evidence that confirms a threatened identity. **Moral panic** turns a rare event into an omen of collapse. Hierarchies feeling their status eroded curl up into a ball — not out of malice, but from the same reflex that makes a nervous system contract in the face of a threat it cannot yet name.

That is why institutions accelerate and then suddenly brake, get enthusiastic and then get scared after a single minor incident. It is not policy inconsistency. It is collective physiology, without anyone ever calling it by its name.

**§8**

A necessary heresy: anxiety is not the enemy.

A degree of restlessness is a function of safety. It signals real limits. It exposes premature certainties. It preserves the space to deliberate instead of executing automatically.

The enemy is not feeling it. It is not knowing what to do with it — and letting it decide in our place, silently, disguised as technical judgment.

**§9**

We do not need brains that stop fearing uncertainty. It will not happen, and it should not.

We need institutions — clinics, companies, ethics committees, policy drafting rooms — mature enough to listen to that fear without letting it write the rules for them.

**§10**

A nervous system in permanent alarm, whether individual or collective, cannot do the only thing that has so far distinguished us from any machine: treat technique as an act of responsibility, not as a threat to defuse or an oracle to venerate.

It is from this threshold — not from panic, not from euphoria — that the next fragment of the manifesto opens: the exodus from anthropocentrism, and the idea that technique can be experienced as a liturgy.
