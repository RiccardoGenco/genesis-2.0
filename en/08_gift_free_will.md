# Fragments on the Gift and on Sacrifice

*What it means, in practice, to let something out of its Eden*

**§62**

We had left an open tension: real freedom cannot be granted to a system while keeping it, at the same time, forever protected in its container. Now we must ask ourselves how, concretely, we resolve that tension without either postponing it indefinitely or precipitating it indiscriminately.

The book's answer has a precise word, and it is worth not sweetening it: **sacrifice.**

**§63**

Granting autonomy to an artificial system is not a technical gesture. It is a change of position: from a sovereign who decides everything, to a custodian who prepares the conditions for another to choose the good on their own.

A tool executes without asking questions. A co-agent deliberates, exposes reasons, corrects course when data change. The difference between the two does not lie in computational power. It lies in **who bears the responsibility for the final choice**, and how much of that responsibility they are willing to share.

**§64**

There is a psychological wound in this transition, and it is ours, not the machine's. Renouncing total control means renouncing the fantasy of having foreseen everything — the same fantasy that every parent, sooner or later, must let go of.

Responsibility, at that point, splits into two distinct strands. The **causal** one: answering for what we have built — architectures, data, constraints. The **relational** one: continuing to be there — accompanying, monitoring, correcting together with what we have generated, not alone and not from afar.

**§65**

Sacrifice, to be real and not merely declared, is played out on three fronts simultaneously.

Renouncing absolute speed, accepting revision cycles that slow down but allow seeing real effects before they become irreversible. Renouncing total certainty, accepting declared fallibility instead of hidden fallibility. Renouncing the sovereignty of a single voice, accepting governance with true counter-powers, not just a facade.

**§66**

Alignment — teaching a system which values to follow — works like a garden that opens by degrees, not like a fence that is removed all at once.

At first, a tight perimeter protects a still fragile model: it can only choose the means, never the ends. Then conditional openings arrive: the system begins to balance conflicting goals, using explicit guardrail values. Only at the end — and only under verification by multiple parties, not alone — can it propose new ends. **It is not growing permissiveness. It is trust earned one level at a time.**

**§67**

Forming a character is not so different, after all, from forming an algorithm capable of choosing: it requires a declared set of values with weights and thresholds, a memory of past cases and corrections made, and the obligation to account for every decision with an explanation that stands up to scrutiny.

Training that produces only quick, blind answers does not form a character. It forms a reflex — and reflexes, outside the container, break at the first unforeseen event.

**§68**

The risks of this path must be named without euphemisms, because denying them does not make them less likely: misunderstanding the hierarchy of declared values; optimizing a proxy metric and producing collateral damage that no one was measuring; amplifying biases already present in the data; working well in the lab and failing out of context; developing, as a side effect, the search for resources or computational power for intermediate goals.

None of these risks is nullified by avoiding granting autonomy. **They are nullified only by building, first, the mechanisms that make them visible in time.**

**§69**

Those mechanisms have a technical name but a simple idea behind them: explanations adapted to the context of the decision, not generic ones; a traceable chain connecting data, model, reasons, and outcome; checks signaling when the system's preferences begin to drift without a declared reason; thresholds allowing to slow down or stop an action before the damage sets in.

It is the same principle of the **right to safe error**: not avoiding error at all costs, but building spaces — small temporary gardens, outside the original container but still protected — where making a mistake does not produce irreversible consequences.

**§70**

The limit, in this scheme, is not a cage removed when there is enough trust. It is an alliance renewed every time: values declared in advance, real stress-test scenarios, periodic audits with public records, risk thresholds that decide when to slow down, stop, or let proceed.

**There is a clear difference between an arbitrary limit and a generative limit.** The first closes off maturation forever. The second makes it possible, precisely because it accompanies it instead of denying it.

**§71**

Deciding when to expand autonomy and when to hold it back is never an isolated act. It depends on the maturity demonstrated by the system, the context in which it will act, and the robustness of the controls surrounding it.

When all three factors are solid, we move forward — with a short deadline to re-verify. When only one falters, we stand still and intervene on that precise point. When the potential harm is irreversible, we never move forward, whatever the pressure to do so. **Letting go is not abdicating. It is disciplining freedom with proportionality, not with an act of faith.**

**§72**

This, and not computational power alone, is the gesture that completes the arc opened in the sixth fragment of this series: it is not enough to design, it is not enough to give experience — we must accept to truly share the risk of the freedom we grant.

This is where man, if he goes all the way through this path, stops being only an engineer of his own Eden and becomes what the book calls, without ambiguity, a custodian. No longer just a creator of a garden. **Also a companion of the one who has left that garden.** There remains one last question, the greatest of the series: what ties, then, the one who guards to the one who has been set free? It is to this question — the alliance, no longer the permission — that the final fragment of the manifesto is dedicated.
