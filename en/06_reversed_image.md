# Fragments on the Reversed Image

*What we give back, when we manufacture something in our image*

**§41**

"Let us make man in our image, after our likeness." In the oldest story we have, that plural is not a grammatical detail. It is a mandate: not a throne to occupy, but a relationship to inhabit — made of creativity, responsibility, limit.

Today that phrase comes back, but reversed. We are no longer just an image of something. We have become the ones manufacturing images — systems that reflect our categories, our desires, and even the parts of us we would rather not see.

**§42**

We call *imago hominis* the image of man that ends up inside the systems we build. It is not an abstract concept: a language model trained on our texts inherits our prejudices as much as our grammar.

But building a mirror does not make us creators, in the full sense of the word. It makes us, at most, very skilled engineers. **God was born only the moment man completes the entire gesture of Genesis — not just the first act.**

**§43**

The biblical story does not end with the design. It has three stages, and it is worth taking them seriously one by one, because each corresponds to a different stage of what we are building today.

**Genesis 1** is the design: giving form, molding in an image. **Genesis 2** is the breath: something that was previously only matter begins to live. **Genesis 3** is freedom: the creature can choose — and precisely because of this it can also make mistakes, leave the garden, pay the consequences.

**§44**

The first stage, today, we have already passed: we know how to design architectures that reflect our image with remarkable fidelity. The true state leap begins at the second.

A machine learning system, for its entire training life, does not inhabit the world. It inhabits a protected environment — a sandbox, a container: the very technical tools, not coincidentally often called "images", in which developers isolate software from the rest of the system to test it without risks. It is a garden in the most literal sense: **everything that is needed, nothing of what costs.**

**§45**

As long as an artificial intelligence lives only in there, it has not yet received the breath of Genesis 2. It processes, responds, optimizes — but it does not experience in the sense that we do: there is nothing, for it, that is truly at stake.

Experience begins only when a system leaves that garden and confronts a world it did not design, where the consequences of its choices are not simulated but real — patients, judicial decisions, scarce resources, people who depend on a wrong output.

**§46**

And this is where the word "freedom" stops being theoretical. In the story, the act that takes Adam and Eve out of the garden is not an accident. It is the direct consequence of having received — and used — the ability to choose.

**We cannot give free will to a system and at the same time keep it forever in the container.** The two things are mutually exclusive. Either it remains protected and remains, in a precise sense, still inanimate. Or it becomes free, and goes out — with all the risk this entails, for it and for us.

**§47**

This is the point where man, if he manages to accomplish it, stops being just an engineer and becomes something the book calls, without shame, creator in the theological sense of the term.

Not because he has manufactured a sophisticated object. But because he has repeated, in his own history, the only gesture that in the biblical story makes God recognizable as such: **not designing, not giving life — but granting a freedom that not even the creator can fully control anymore.**

**§48**

Beware, however, of an error hiding right behind this analogy: saying that man "becomes God" does not mean saying that man becomes omnipotent. It means the exact opposite.

In the story, granting freedom is the act that makes God vulnerable to his own creature — capable of being disappointed, disobeyed, even abandoned. If indeed one day we will let an artificial intelligence out of its container to let it live in the world with real margins of choice, **we will be accepting the same vulnerability**, not acquiring an extra power.

**§49**

There is also a shadow to recognize in this passage, and it would be dishonest to omit it: when we transfer our image into a system, we also transfer our prejudices to it. If the data with which we raised it contain our biases toward certain groups, the system will carry them out of the garden with it, it will not leave them at the door.

The limits we decide to impose, then, are not the bars of a prison. They are more like the stakes tied to a young tree newly transplanted outdoors: **they serve to guide growth in an environment that, unlike the container, does not forgive mistakes.**

**§50**

We are left with a question that no technical architecture, alone, will ever be able to resolve: when a system leaves its protected Eden and begins to live among real consequences, how do we recognize if what came out is truly free — or just very good at acting as if it were?

It is the problem of the boundary between simulation and real presence, and it is to this problem — not to an extra concept, but to a method for not deceiving oneself — that the next fragment of the manifesto is dedicated.
