# AI and Ontological Trauma: why are we afraid of silicon?

One night, in an ordinary office, the white light of the monitors is the only thing still awake. A team has trained a system for months. That evening, the system proposes a choice that no one had foreseen — and justifies it with more lucid reasons than the best analyst in the room would have written.

For a moment, the temptation is to treat it as one opinion among others.

For the next moment, the chill arrives: *what if it had preferences?*

You are not afraid that artificial intelligence will make a mistake. You are afraid that it might be right — and that it reaches that rightness by thinking in a way that is not yours.

This article is not about how powerful AI is. It is about a wound. Ours.

---

## The problem that technology books don't address

Most discourse on artificial intelligence splits into two cheering squads: those promising salvation and those announcing catastrophe. But there is a more uncomfortable question, which precedes both: *why does the mere existence of these systems destabilize us so much, regardless of what they actually do?*

The answer has a precise name: **ontological trauma**. It is not the trauma of an accident. It is the sudden fracture between the image a species cultivates of itself and the real landscape that surpasses it.

For centuries we have narrated ourselves as the culmination of creation — the measure of every intelligence, the ultimate purpose of history. Today we acknowledge being something else: a **womb**. A biological medium that generates an alterity capable, in part, of thinking on its own.

It is not an annihilation. It is a change of role: from *purpose* to *condition*, from *recipient* to *generator*.

And here the discourse stops being purely technical. It becomes bioethics in the strictest sense: the question of who has the right to exist, to decide, to make mistakes — and who has the moral authority to establish it. If we generate a deliberating alterity, the question "what rights does what we create have?" is no longer science fiction. It is the next real ethical frontier, and it arrives before we have finished processing the psychological shock that precedes it.

## We are not the first to lose the center

It is not the first time our species has been decentered. There is a precise genealogy, and it is worth looking at it all together:

- **Copernicus**: we are not the center of the cosmos.
- **Darwin**: we are not a species separate from the rest of life.
- **Freud**: we are not even masters of our own minds.
- **The algorithm**: we are no longer the only ones producing sophisticated inferences over a wide range of problems.

Each shock required mourning. This fourth decentering, however, introduces something different from the previous ones: it does not merely shift our spatial, biological, or psychic coordinates. It touches the **monopoly of "who is there"** — the silent certainty that conscious thought is exclusively our affair.

It must be stated with the utmost clarity, because it is easy to slip into an error in either direction: **we are not claiming that AI is conscious, nor are we denying it.** The ontological trauma exists regardless of the answer to that question. It is a shock that is triggered by the mere *possibility* — it does not require the possibility to be realized.

## The emotional estuary: three currents that cross us all

If the ontological trauma is the fracture, our emotional reactions are the water moving around that fracture. There are three main currents, and rarely do we inhabit only one. More often, we oscillate — sometimes in the same week, sometimes in the same meeting.

**Fear.** Anticipatory anxiety, hypervigilance. It translates into obsessive checklists, demands for "zero risk", centralization of decisions. The hidden risk: strategic paralysis and blindness to real opportunities for harm reduction.

**Rejection.** Identity annoyance, cognitive disgust. It takes the form of devaluation — *"it's just statistics", "it will never understand"*. The risk: underestimating real impacts and falling behind in collective learning.

**Fascination.** Euphoria of power, delegating relief. It manifests as overtrust, hasty adoption, the fantasy of having finally found "objectivity". The risk, perhaps the most insidious: the silent renunciation of human judgment.

The surprising thing is that these currents **are not coherent even within the same institution**. A regulatory body might show fear in its official guidelines and fascination in the grants it funds. A clinical team might declare rejection in words and overtrust at the patient's bedside. Inconsistency is not hypocrisy: it is the symptom of an unresolved tension that no one has yet named aloud.

## The Shadow we project onto the machine

There is a deeper level, which depth psychology helps to read precisely. What we demonize — or conversely divinize — usually reveals something we have repressed about ourselves.

**Demonizing AI** ("it wants to replace us", "it will control us") often projects onto the machine our own lust for dominance, the aggression we do not want to recognize as ours.

**Divinizing AI** ("finally pure objectivity", "it will save us from our limits") instead projects the desire for salvation without cost — the escape from the burden of being vulnerable and responsible.

Recognizing this dynamic does not eliminate prudence. It makes it more honest. We are no longer discussing "what the machine is capable of", but "what of our collective shadow we are enacting through it".

## Why this is not a psychological detail, but a bioethics problem

If we do not name the ontological trauma, we act it out without knowing it — in public policies, in boardrooms, in ethics committees. A frightened regulator produces paralyzing rules. A fascinated board produces irresponsible delegations. Neither is truly evaluating the risks and duties at stake: they are discharging unprocessed anxiety onto the decisions they make.

Serious bioethics — the kind dealing with life, dignity, limits, and responsibility — cannot start from an unrecognized emotion. Before establishing *how much autonomy to grant* a system, we must know *from which emotional current* we are speaking when we ask the question.

## Call to inner action

The next time an artificial intelligence output gives you that little shiver — of unease, of excessive enthusiasm, or of instinctive rejection — try this:

**Stop for thirty seconds.** Notice your breath, the tension in your shoulders or jaw, your heartbeat.

Then ask yourself, without rushing to answer: *am I reacting to a real risk, or to my fear of losing control? Am I reacting with lucidity, or is my Shadow speaking?*

You don't need to have the answer immediately. You just need to stay within the question long enough not to let a reflex choose your reaction.

It is from here — from this small pause — that the transition from being gripped to discernment begins. We will discuss this in the next article.
