# Fragments on the Vow and the False Dilemma

*Why safety is not optimization, and the algorithm is not an oracle*

**§21**

We have dismantled the device of control. Dismantling it is not enough: we must give it another form to inhabit, or it will rebuild itself, faster than before.

The form is called creative liturgy. It is not a religious rite disguised as governance. It is a deliberate sequence of acts, norms, and symbols that converts energy, attention, and knowledge into something that was not there before: more shared reality.

**§22**

"More shared reality" is not a vague statement. It means three measurable things: more true things made public, more lives made possible and less exposed to harm, more justice in distributions and reparations.

It is not an end imposed from above — no one has decreed it by law. It is an **ethical attractor**: a pattern recognized after the fact in systems that open instead of close, that make things reversible instead of irrevocable, that protect the vulnerable instead of ignoring them. Call it weak teleology, if you want a technical name. Or call it, more simply, the only direction that can still be corrected.

**§23**

To renounce the center, we said. But renouncing is not an abstract gesture: it is a vow, and a vow always has two bodies.

The psychic body: to stop demanding ontological centrality, to shift the axis from the ego to the bond.

The political body: to convert power into custody — establishing limits, accountability, renunciations that distribute action instead of concentrating it.

**The vow does not reduce effectiveness. It redefines it.** The goal ceases to be total control, it becomes relational fecundity — measurable in resilience, not in dominance.

**§24**

Four pillars support this form, and none of the four is optional:

- **Declared intention** — purposes and success criteria made public before, not justified after the fact.
- **Shared form** — transparent protocols, verifiable by those who did not write them.
- **Offering and renunciation** — visible trade-offs, accepted slowdowns when they protect those who are most exposed.
- **Memory and promise** — documented errors, verifiable commitments over time, not declared once and forgotten.

A system that respects three out of four is not "almost safe". It is a system that has already chosen which pillar to sacrifice when it really counts.

**§25**

Imagine an engineer, a neo-mystic devotee, and someone trying to stand between the two.

The engineer says: safety is optimization. Give us clean data, a well-specified loss function, access to computation. Rules slow down innovation.

The devotee says: the algorithm knows. Its correlations are oracles. Let it speak without veils or bureaucracies — it will guide us beyond our miseries.

The one standing in the middle answers both: **you confuse means and ends.** The first absolutizes performance. The second transfigures it into revelation. In neither case is responsibility established. In both, it is simply delegated to something else.

**§26**

The engineer insists: value is measurable, KPIs are clear, the rest is rhetoric.

But **the metric decides what exists.** If you optimize only accuracy, you can amplify harms that no one is measuring: unfair distributions, irreversible errors that do not appear on any dashboard. Limits are not the opposite of freedom. They are the place where freedom becomes experiential without becoming a catastrophe.

**§27**

The devotee retorts: why fear the oracle? Enough with guilt — let us surrender.

This is not faith. It is **deresponsibilization with a more elegant vocabulary.** Fetishizing the machine as numinous in itself removes the only duty that counts: justifying choices before those who suffer the consequences. The required transparency is not total unveiling — it is a publishable reason: sufficient for a competent third party to evaluate purposes, assumptions, trade-offs.

**§28**

Real risks, however, are not hypothetical, and must be named without discounts.

**Regulatory capture** — when those who should be controlled write the rules that control them.

**Theatricalization of ethics** — compliance reduced to a facade, without ever touching the decisions that really matter.

**Overtrust from the aura of the sacred** — the mirror error of technocratic fetishism: trusting a system not because it is verified, but because it is cloaked in a language that sounds ethical.

Countermeasures exist, and they are concrete: rotation of reviewers, stress tests of conflicting values before deployment, simulations with adversarial parties, audits published with reasoned responses — not filed away in a drawer.

**§29**

And if, one day, what we have built were to start claiming a point of view of its own? Would it not be impious, then, to continue to set limits on it?

No — provided that the limits remain an open hypothesis, not a dogma defended regardless of the evidence. Until robust proof, autonomy remains tied to a weak teleology and cycles of intersubjective revision. If serious markers of subjectivity emerge, constraints will have to change form — not out of fear, but out of consistency: the form serves to adapt to facts, not to protect us from them.

**§30**

We have given a name to the form — liturgy — and dismantled its poisonous double, technocracy and superstition combined.

There remains a question that no dialogue, however well-constructed, can resolve alone: **what does the evidence really say, today, about the possibility that there is someone, and not just something, on the other side?** Not an opinion. Traces. It is there that the next fragment of the manifesto opens.
